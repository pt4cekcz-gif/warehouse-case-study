From $18 Million of Deadstock to a Lean, High-Performance Warehouse

How a leading manufacturer eliminated chronic inventory imbalances, overhauled its warehouse layout, and successfully migrated to a new ERP — cutting non-moving stock by 30% and boosting picking productivity by 20%.

Background

A large manufacturing company found itself caught in a paradox familiar to many operations managers: its warehouse was simultaneously overstuffed and understocked. Shelves groaned under the weight of slow-moving components nobody needed, while the parts that kept production lines running were consistently nowhere to be found.

Making matters worse, the company was preparing for a major technology transition — migrating its entire enterprise resource planning system from a legacy JBA platform to a modern solution. The stakes were high. A botched migration could compound existing inefficiencies, amplify data quality issues, and expose the organization to serious operational risk.

"Over $18 million in non-moving parts were clogging warehouse space, while 15% of the most critical components were regularly out of stock — a dual crisis of waste and scarcity."

The Problem

The challenges facing this manufacturer were deeply interconnected. At the surface level, the warehouse looked busy — but that busyness masked profound inefficiency.

Key Challenges Identified
  • Over $18 million in non-moving "D" category parts occupied valuable storage space with no clear path to use or disposal
  • 15% of "A" category critical parts — those essential to daily production — were frequently out of stock, causing delays
  • The existing ERP system contained poor-quality data, presenting a significant risk ahead of the planned migration
  • The physical warehouse layout had never been optimized around actual inventory movement patterns, leading to inefficient picking routes
  • Supply chain teams operated in functional silos with weak cross-department communication, preventing coordinated decision-making

The root cause was a lack of structured inventory strategy. Without a formal classification framework, all parts were treated roughly equally — meaning fast-moving essentials competed for space with years-old surplus. The result was a warehouse that was full but not functional.

The Approach

Caresoft Global's consultants began with a comprehensive diagnostic: mapping inventory movement, auditing data quality, and interviewing stakeholders across procurement, operations, and logistics. What emerged was a clear picture of misalignment between how the warehouse was organized and how it actually needed to function.

The intervention unfolded in parallel workstreams:

Solution Framework
  • Plan for Every Part (PFEP): A master data document was created for each SKU, capturing usage rates, supplier lead times, and optimal storage locations
  • ABC Classification: All inventory was segmented by movement frequency — "A" parts (fast-moving, critical) were repositioned to prime picking locations; "D" parts were flagged for disposition
  • Warehouse Layout Redesign: Physical slotting was restructured so that high-frequency picks required the least travel distance, dramatically reducing picker walk time
  • SOPs & Visual Management: Standardized operating procedures were introduced alongside visual cues — floor markings, labels, signage — to reduce errors and support new staff onboarding
  • ERP Migration Support: Data cleansing was carried out before migration, user acceptance testing was structured, and teams received hands-on training on the new system

The Results

The transformation delivered measurable gains across the key dimensions of the problem. Within the project timeline, the warehouse moved from reactive firefighting to a structured, data-driven operation.

Measured Outcomes
  • Value of non-moving "D" parts reduced by 30%, freeing up capital and reclaiming storage space for productive use
  • Picking productivity increased by 20%, driven by improved slotting and clearer operational workflows
  • ERP migration completed successfully, underpinned by improved data quality and structured change management
  • A continuous improvement foundation was established, giving the internal team tools and methods to sustain gains long-term

Key Takeaways

This case study illustrates that warehouse transformation doesn't require a complete technology overhaul to achieve significant results. The most impactful changes here were analytical and organizational: classifying inventory properly, restructuring physical space around actual demand patterns, and ensuring data integrity before a system migration.

The combination of a Plan for Every Part with ABC classification is a proven methodology — but its power lies in execution. When every stakeholder understands how parts are categorized and why certain items live in certain locations, the whole system becomes more legible, more efficient, and more resilient to disruption.

"The most powerful intervention wasn't technological — it was bringing structure, visibility, and shared language to inventory that had grown invisible through familiarity."

For organizations facing similar challenges — ERP transitions, chronic stockouts alongside excess inventory, or warehouse layouts that have grown organically without strategic intent — this case offers a clear template: start with the data, classify your inventory, redesign around movement, and build processes that sustain the gains.
